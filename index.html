Parkrun: Who has access, and who participates? - Analysis and policy recommendations

Schneider PP1, Smith R1, Brennan A1, Haake S2, Goyder E1

1 School of Health and Related Research, University of Sheffield, Sheffield, UK
2 Advanced Wellbeing Research Centre, Sheffield Hallam University, SheffieldS, UK

- Work in Progress -


Motivation

Insufficient physical activity is one of the leading causes of disease and disability worldwide (WHO, 2018). For the UK, it has been estimated that one in six deaths is attributable to insufficient physical activity (Lee et al.Â 2012). Increasing the physical activity levels of the UK population has the potential to improve quality of life, reduce mortality rates and alleviate the strain on health and social care services.

However, interventions to promote physical activity can have unintended consequences: health promotion activities are often fail to reach those who need it the most. Socio-economically deprived groups are frequently underrepresented, evidence suggst that they may also achieve poorer behaviour change outcomes following interventions. If not carfully designed, interventions to increase the levels of physcial activity could worsen the already stark health inequalities in the UK.

Parkrun, a grass-roots running movement which originated in Bushy Park, London, has been widely praised as being successful in encouraging participation in groups which are particularly inactive and often underrepresented in health promotion activities (Stevenson et al., 2014). Participants report that accessibility and inclusivity are among the main factors that contributed to their initial attendance and sustained involvement (Stevinson et al., 2017). These early findings seems to suggest that the parkrun movement appears to hold huge potential from a public health perspective, to increase physical activiy, whithout increasing inequalities in health.

The present study aims to provide more insights into who has access to parkrun events in England, and who participates. It goes on to identify sites for future parkrun events to improve (equitable) public access.


What is Parkrun? Parkrun hosts five-kilometre running events in public parks at 9am every Saturday. The events are organized by volunteers and participation is free. As of October 2018, there were 570 parkrun locations in the UK, and 1.8 million registered runners. For more info, see Wikipedia


Research question

This study aims to investigate the (potential) geographic access to parkrun events and the actual utilization. Based on our findings, we provide policy recommendation for increasing both. More specifically, we assessed:

  1. Who has access to parkrun events in England? We looked at the relationship between the deprivation of an area and its geographic distance to the nearest parkrun event, to assess whether people in more deprived areas need to travel longer distances to participate.

  2. Who participates in parkrun events? We analysed participant data from parkrun events from 2018 to see from which areas participants of parkrun events actually came: Are people from more deprived areas underrepreented? Do people from more affluent areas travel longer distances to attend running events?

  3. We considered what we have learned from questions one and two and provide some recommendations for locations for future parkrun events, in order to maximize (equitable) public access or utilization. We also identified parkrun events that were particularly successful in attracting people from more deprived areas, as potential case studies for future investigations.
